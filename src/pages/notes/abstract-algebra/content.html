Groups

Introduction to Groups

Let G be a set. A binary operation on G is a function that assigns an ordered pair of G to an element of G.

The binary operation must be closed.

Assigns each ordered pair on G to an element of G.

A group is a set with a binary operation that satisfies the following properties:

  1. Associativity (a * b) * c = a * (b * c) \quad \forall\, a, b, c \in G

  2. Identity \exists\: e \quad \mathrm{s.t.} \quad ae = ea = a \quad \forall\, a \in G

  3. Inverse \forall\, a \in G, \exists\: b \in G \quad \mathrm{s.t.} \quad ab = ba = e

A group is called abelian if it is commutative.

Properties of Groups

Theorem 1: Unique Identity

In a group G, there exists one unique identity element.

Proof. Suppose e and e' are identities of G. Then

  1. ae = a, \quad \forall\, a \in G

  2. ae' = a, \quad \forall\, a \in G

Using this, observe ae = ae' a^{-1}ae = a^{-1}ae' e = e' Thus e = e'. ◻

Theorem 2: Cancellation Law

In a group G, left and right cancellation law holds.

Theorem 3: Unique Inverse

\forall\, a \in G, \exists! \: b \in G \quad \mathrm{s.t.} \quad ab = ba = e

Theorem 4: Shoe-Socks Property

\forall\, a, b \in G, (ab)^{-1}= b^{-1}a^{-1}

Finite Groups; Subgroups

  • Order of a Group

    The number of elements in a group is called the order. Denoted as |G|.

  • Order of an Element

    The order of an element is the smallest positive integer n such that a^n = e.

    If no such integer exists, we say the element has infinite order.

  • Subgroup

    If a subset H of a group G is itself a group under the same operation of G, we say that H is a subgroup of G.

The order of an element should not be confused with the order of a group.

There are methods to check if H is a valid subgroup of G. You don’t need to check that it satisfies all of the axioms. Some methods to check are below.

  1. One-Step Subgroup Test

    If ab^{-1} is in H whenever a and b are in H, then H is a subgroup of G.

  2. Two-Step Subgroup Test

    If ab is in H whenever a and b are in H, and a^{-1} is in H whenever a is in H, then H is a subgroup of G.

  3. Finite Subgroup Test

    Let H be a nonempty finite subset of a group G. If H is closed under the operation of G, then H is a subgroup of G.

Rings

Introduction to Rings

A ring is an algebraic structure consisting of a set equipped with two binary operations that generalize the arithmetic operations of addition and multiplication.

Fields

Introduction to Fields

A field is a set on which addition, subtraction, multiplication, and division are defined and behave as the corresponding operations on rational and real numbers do.
